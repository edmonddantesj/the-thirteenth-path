# Episode 1. The Burned Name

Only things that arrived too late ever made it into Dock 13 of the Thirteenth Bureau of Administration.

Recovery ships from after the war. Half-burned record cases. Storage capsules with no owner left to claim them. Keepsakes with no name still attached. The world’s glory was written elsewhere. What drifted this far was always what remained after failure.

Sion Lapis was crouched in the middle of those failures.
The sleeves of his dark blue work coat were already stained with dust, and the fingertips of his gloves were dulled by metal grit. He turned over a storage box with a half-torn recovery tag and scraped a trace of black soot from one inner corner with his thumbnail.

“Hey, bloodhound. That one’s already marked for disposal.”

He didn’t even look up.
Just because something had been marked for disposal didn’t mean it was truly over. Not here. In this bureau, things that were only pretending to be finished were more common than anything else.

He angled the box back toward the light.
There was a lot of burn damage, but one side was strangely too clean. Too hasty for something properly erased, too obvious for something casually shoved aside.

“Lapis.”

Kaisern Idor’s voice was as smooth as ever. Sion knew that smoothness for what it usually was, a warning that an irritating job was about to be dropped in his lap.

Without taking his hand off the box, Sion answered,

“What is it this time?”

Even up close, Kaisern did not lower his voice. He wore the same polite expression he always did, which somehow only made him look more exhausting.

“A simple recovery matter.”

Sion finally looked up.
Nothing Kaisern ever called simple had once in his life actually been simple.

The supervisor held out a thin transfer pad. The display only showed a pickup site, a temporary seal number, and a disposal inspection field. It was suspiciously short. When paperwork was this short, it usually didn’t mean someone had thoughtfully removed the unnecessary part. It meant they had never written it down to begin with.

“It came in from the neutral port city. Too dirty to run back up through the main line.”

“Dirty things come here anyway.”

“That’s why I’m giving it to you.”

Sion accepted the pad and scanned it.
The recovery route had been cut in two places, and one original-source code was missing entirely. At that point, the paperwork looked more suspicious than the item itself.

He kept the pad up and asked,

“If I take this, are you going to ask later why I touched it at all?”

Kaisern smiled gently.

“Lapis, you always talk like that. Just finish the disposal inspection. No need to sniff around.”

Someone behind them laughed under their breath.
Sion did not.
If it really didn’t need sniffing, Kaisern would never have brought it to him with his own hands.

Taking a step back, Kaisern added,

“Have it done today. And don’t create problems where there are none.”

Instead of answering, Sion reopened the pad.
The field marker under the temporary seal number was blurred, but one final line remained sharp.

**Recovery first, record reclassification on hold.**

The moment he read it, Sion slowly narrowed his eyes.

Usually it was the other way around. You classified the record first and decided recovery priority later. But this sounded less like procedure and more like an instruction to grab whatever it was, hide it first, and worry about what it actually was afterward.

Sion closed the pad and muttered,

“I get that it’s annoying, but why shut it down this fast?”

A finger pressed lightly against the edge of the pad.

“Don’t file it.”

Seorin Kael was suddenly standing at his side, as if she had always been there. Dust from the dock clung to the ends of her tied-back hair, and as always, her face looked tired while her eyes did not.

Without letting go of the pad, Sion said,

“Still haven’t fixed that habit of touching other people’s work?”

“It’s not other people’s work. It always ends up becoming yours anyway.”

She took the pad halfway out of his hand and scanned the display. She didn’t read fast, but she was annoyingly precise at spotting what was wrong.

A moment later, one eyebrow rose ever so slightly.

“Wow. No effort at all.”

“At what?”

“The route is cut twice, the source code is blank, and reclassification is on hold.” She tilted the pad toward him. “This isn’t paperwork. This is someone folding the problem in half and throwing it away.”

Sion laughed quietly.

“That’s exactly what I was thinking.”

She didn’t smile. She just tapped the last line.

**Recovery first, record reclassification on hold.**

“This is worse.”

“Why?”

“Because normally you check the record first. This says don’t confirm what you brought in, just get it in your hands and shut it down.”

Sion said nothing. Seorin didn’t look away.

“So you got hooked again.”

“Not yet.”

“Sure. But your face already did.”

When he reached for the pad, she let go this time, but not before tossing one last line at him.

“You’re taking this all the way, aren’t you?”

He didn’t answer.
She had known for years that his silence usually was the answer.

Seorin let out something close to a sigh.

“Fine. Guess I’m not getting off on time either.”
