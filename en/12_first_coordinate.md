# Episode 12. The First Coordinate

Elia stared at the fragment for a long while without speaking.

The room's quietness had not changed, but the grain of the silence had. The silence when the door first opened had been the kind that measured people. The silence now was much closer to everyone waiting only for Elia's fingertips. Under the old desk lamp, the severed edge, the burned rim, and the remaining piece of the name were beginning, very slowly, to show another face.

Elia placed the fragment on the reading plate and spread a very thin filter membrane over it with her other hand.
Faint lines bloomed in the air. Dead sentences. Broken signature lines. Record traces warped by heat. It looked less like a document than like a wound. Yet Elia did not look like a woman reading text. She looked like someone reading where the wound had first been cut.

Sion could not even fold his arms as he stood there.
Seorin glanced at him and said softly,

"You're holding your breath right now."

"I'm not."

"Liar."

Even that brief exchange had no real strength in it this time.
Sion's gaze stayed fixed on the reading plate.

Elia murmured low,

"Not one fragment. Two."

Sern reacted at once.

"Two."

"Yeah."
Elia answered without even looking up.
"The erased sentence is one, but the remaining trace splits in two directions. One is toward the verdict text. The other is... hm."

She pushed up one side of the filter membrane with her fingertip, and a much fainter line surfaced on the plate. It looked closer to a route marker than to a sentence. Even so, it was so incomplete that anyone else would have dismissed it as one more scratch or ten more layers of noise.

"That's not a sentence."
Seorin said it first.

"No, it isn't."
Elia replied.
"It's a path."

Sion slowly narrowed his eyes.

"A route?"

"Not a complete one."
Elia said as she pressed on the shard again.
"Closer to a closed path.
More exactly, an erased path."

At that line, Ater stepped closer for the first time.
This time his face did not belong to a man still minding protocol or distance.
A path. A closed path. An erased path.
In the world of House Valkar and the Imperial Approval Bureau, those were words as sensitive as a person's name.

As though she already knew that, Elia added with perfect calm,

"If only a verdict fragment had remained, this much wouldn't show up.
Whoever hid this didn't just cut away the sentence. They cut away the path with it."

Sern asked in a low voice,

"Does that mean the records keeper received the path as well?"

Elia gave a small shrug.

"Don't know yet whether it reached the records keeper, or whether the records keeper was simply the last one to catch hold of it.
But at minimum, this isn't a trace that stuck there by accident."

The faint line on the reading plate trembled once again.
Elia drew out a smaller lens from the desk drawer and set it over the filter membrane.
Then at the very tip of the line, something like a severed number marker and one old feline-clan style notation flashed into life for an instant.

Sion swallowed.

"That..."

Instead of answering, Elia removed the lens and shut the display off.
The faint light died, and the room sank back into humid reality.

"You can't go around carrying all of this with you now."

Seorin lifted one brow.

"Nice. We know that too."

"No. Not on that level."
Elia looked up.
"What just surfaced wasn't only residue from a verdict text.
It was residue from a path someone tried to erase."

A short silence.

The moment those words landed, Sion felt the weight of the fragment in his hand change ever so slightly.
This was no longer the problem of a name alone.
Someone had not just severed the name. They had cut away the path that name should have reached.
And a path like that was not something you recovered from one sheet of text. It was the kind of thing you only restored by stepping again onto the places from which it had been cut.

Ater lowered his gaze for a brief second.
The path is more dangerous.
To someone from the Approval Bureau, that sentence belonged to an old and very familiar truth. And yet, in this moment, it rang with an entirely different meaning.

Sern asked quietly,

"How much of it can be restored."

Elia did not answer at once.
Instead, she looked over the fragment, the reading plate, and the four faces around her.
The very possibility of restoration was already expensive information. And what these four had brought in now was something more dangerous than money.

"A full restoration isn't possible yet."
She said.
"But we have the first fragment."

Sion asked at once,

"Where."

Elia tapped the short notation she had just written at the edge of the reading plate with her thumbnail.

"A closed transfer point in the outer cluster.
The name will have changed by now, and in official records it will probably remain as little more than a dead place."
She added briefly,
"It used to be an auxiliary transfer point where cargo and people pushed to the margins were split and rerouted. After the war, it turned into a place that knew too much, so it survived by pretending to be dead."

Seorin muttered low,

"Nice. Now we even have a destination."

"Not a destination. A first point of verification."
Elia corrected her.
"The answer may not be waiting there outright.
But there's a strong chance the next trace of whoever erased the path will be there."
Elia tapped the reading plate again with her fingers.
"You can carry a fragment in your hand, but the answer always stays longer in the place the fragment was cut from."

Sion listened, then slipped the shard back into his coat.
Until now, they had been the side running.
Now, for the first time, a place they had to move toward existed.

The one who noticed that subtle difference first was Seorin.

"Nice."
She said in a flat voice.
"Now we're not just the side being chased anymore."

Elia gave a small laugh.

"No. The side being chased still fits better."
Then she added at once,
"You just finally know where to run if you want to arrive before it's too late."

From the doorway, Yona folded his arms.

"If it's a closed transfer point in the outer cluster, fuel gets more expensive."

Sion let out a short laugh.

"Would've been weird if you hadn't said that."

Ater kept looking at Elia.
What she did was not restore records.
She read the things that had never been fully erased, the severed paths, the cut sequences, the traces that remained behind. If the Imperial Approval Bureau managed results, then this place read the hesitations and blanks cut out from inside those results.

Very quietly, he asked,

"If we go to that transfer point, will the next fragment appear?"

Elia gave a slight tilt of her shoulder.

"If you're lucky."
And then, in the same plain voice, she added,
"If you're unlucky, you'll be buried there with it."

The room fell silent for a moment.
No one heard that as bluster. Elia was not the type to exaggerate just to scare people. She priced danger exactly as it stood.

Sern was the first to move back into the practical.

"Who knows these coordinates."

"For now, me."
Elia said.
"And now, you."

Seorin laughed briefly.

"Nice. Our names are on it again."

"No."
Elia corrected.
"From here on, the path is on it too."

That line landed, and the room grew quiet once more.
Until a moment ago, they had been holding onto an erased name. Now they had to follow the path that had been severed along with it. No one yet knew the true name of that path.
But one thing was clear.
The case had moved beyond restoring one person. It was now crossing into the pursuit of erased routes and closed lines.

Closing the reading plate, Elia said at last,

"Good.
Then now you really do have to choose."

"Choose what."
Sion asked.

Elia placed the fragment at the center of the desk and looked from one face to the next.

"Whether you'll only keep running.
Or whether you'll go after the first fragment."

Seorin already wore the face of someone who knew the answer, and Sion was halfway there too.
Sern was waiting for his lord's decision, and Ater was pressing his own thoughts quietly forward somewhere in between.

Outside, the harbor noise still had not fully died, and this storage room would never be safe forever.
But at least at this moment, what lay before the four of them was no longer mere survival. It was direction.

And that direction
was starting to force a different question:
not only who had erased the name,
but who had tried to erase the path with it.
