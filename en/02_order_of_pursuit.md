# Episode 2. The Order of Pursuit

The Seventh Reading Chamber of the Imperial Approval Office was always too quiet.

Out in the corridor, a single footstep could echo for a long time. Inside, sound died faster instead. The record plates aligned along the walls, the sealing devices, the approval terminals, all of them were immaculate, and because of that they felt even colder.

Seorn Barek was the kind of man who noticed the smallest disturbance in that silence first. He did so now.

One line in a list of temporary recovery routes flickered very briefly above the reading table. Too short to call an error. Too deliberate to dismiss as accident when it happened twice.

Seorn froze the display and glanced to the side.

Ater Valkar stood opposite him. Ordered uniform. Ordered posture. Ordered silence. He was always like that. Not someone who grabbed at things in haste, but someone who looked first at how far they had already spread.

“One outer-route recovery line has been cut abnormally.”

Ater did not answer at once. Instead, he scrolled through the display with the tips of his fingers. Two gaps in the recovery route. A reassigned temporary seal number. A slight conflict in access traces. It was the texture of something erased in haste, covered in haste, and touched again in haste.

“Which route?”

“The neutral port city line.”

That was when Ater’s hand finally stopped.

Seorn did not miss the moment. It was brief, but real. For the span of a breath, his lord had stopped thinking forward and simply stared.

“It is not an ordinary disposal matter,” Seorn added. “A reading line passed through first, and a sealing line covered it afterward. The order is wrong.”

Ater closed the display in silence.

The Imperial Approval Office trusted order above all else. What was opened, what was closed, what was erased, what was left behind. That entire system began with sequence. Which meant that when sequence itself was distorted, it was worse than an ordinary mistake.

“Prepare an approach line to the site.”

Seorn bowed his head slightly.

“Official inspection, or unofficial verification?”

Before answering, Ater let his gaze settle once on the smooth face of the closed record plate. Under the polished surface, the trace of something hurriedly removed still lingered.

“For now, verification.”

Seorn stored those words, for now, away with care. Ater always used exact language. And exact language, with him, usually arrived half a step after the real judgment had already formed.

“Understood.”

Even after Seorn withdrew, Ater did not move right away.
The neutral port city. An abnormally severed recovery line. A reading trace reopened after a sealing line had already passed over it.

This matter did not yet have a name.
But matters that were cut down before they could be named usually left behind much larger names later.

At last Ater turned.

“Seorn.”

“Yes, my lord.”

“Find out who reached the site first.”

Seorn paused.

“The Administration Bureau?”

Ater neither denied nor confirmed it. He only said, very quietly,

“One unstructured hand appears to have entered first.”

Seorn asked nothing more. He turned back to the terminal. Ater rarely used a phrase like unstructured. And when he did, it usually meant the matter was about to become far more troublesome than a simple seal.

He pulled up the outer-access list he kept outside the main approval line.
There was more than one road into the neutral port city. Formal envoy routes, imperial logistics approval lines, civilian contract transfers, and gray routes that left no proper record at all. What mattered was which of them had moved first.

As Seorn scanned the list, his brow tightened ever so slightly.

There was no formal movement trace from the Administration Bureau.

That absence meant one of two things. Either they had not moved yet, or they had already slipped outside the official record.

Seorn judged the latter more likely.

“My lord.”

“Speak.”

“The Bureau likely did not take an official route.”

Ater turned to him.

“On what basis?”

“On the absence itself.”

It was a short answer, which made it all the more precise.

“If the paperwork shook this badly, there should be a formal movement trace. There isn’t. If they moved, they likely pretended to wait and slipped out first.”

Ater’s gaze sank slightly.
It was a look in which irritation and interest overlapped very thinly.

“That is not good.”

“No.”

“But if they were first to tamper with the site, then they also left traces.”

Seorn nodded.
When an unstructured hand reached a scene before an ordered one, it always left something behind. An unexpected mark. Sometimes even a door just before it shut.

Ater adjusted the hem of his coat.

“We are going to the neutral port city.”

“What level of escort line shall I prepare?”

“The fastest we can manage without revealing ourselves.”

Seorn did not ask again.
Move under the name of Valkar, but leave as little of Valkar’s shadow as possible.
That alone was enough to prove this matter was not ordinary.

The recovery ship’s interior was narrower than expected.

The moment the door shut, it became obvious the vessel had not been built for carrying people. The ceiling was too low, the reinforcements were rough, and the smell of stale fuel and metal dust hung in the air. One side of the cargo space was stacked with containers marked for disposal, while in the far corner several shadows huddled together in silence, impossible to classify at a glance.

Sion did not trust silence in places like this.
In places where no one spoke, it usually meant everyone had already brought too much of their own trouble aboard.

Seorin listened to the door lock into place and said under her breath,

“Nice. The trash is getting better treatment than the people.”

“Then it’s the right compartment for us.”

“You really do have a habit of talking yourself down.”

“I’m not lowering myself. I’m matching the category.”

“That’s even worse.”

Without smiling, Sion swept his gaze over the cargo hold.
Outwardly it was a waste-recovery ship, but the air inside was too complicated for that. If it had really been carrying nothing but refuse, it would not have been this quiet. The way everyone avoided meeting anyone else’s eyes was far too practiced. Dock workers, brokers, stowaways, or something halfway between. In the end, only one thing mattered.

**No one in here asked anyone else’s name.**

The ship began to vibrate as it pulled away.
Leaning one shoulder against the wall, Seorin asked,

“So. Do you actually have a plan?”

“When we get into the city, we start from the route origin.”

“That’s not a plan. That’s what anyone would say.”

“The point where the record was cut, the transfer belt, the dock number where it changed hands. Then we find the person tied to it.”

Seorin frowned.

“The person?”

Sion shrugged after a brief pause.

“If they burned it and carved it out in this much haste, then it probably wasn’t only an object they were trying to hide.”

“The record handler?”

“Maybe. Or the courier. Or the middleman. Somebody saw something directly.”

Seorin straightened from the wall.

“Great.
So in the end we’re looking for a person.”

“That’s how things usually get bigger.”

“No. They get bigger because of you.”

This time Sion actually smiled, if only a little.
