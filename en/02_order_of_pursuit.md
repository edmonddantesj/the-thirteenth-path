# Episode 2. The Order of Pursuit

The Seventh Reading Chamber of the Imperial Approval Office was always too quiet.

Out in the corridor, a single footstep could echo for a long time. Inside, sound died faster instead. The record plates aligned along the walls, the sealing devices, the approval terminals, all of them were immaculate, and because of that they felt even colder.

Seorn Barek was the kind of man who noticed the smallest disturbance in that silence first. He did so now.

One line in a list of temporary recovery routes flickered very briefly above the reading table. Too short to call an error. Too deliberate to dismiss as accident when it happened twice.

Seorn froze the display and glanced to the side.

Ater Valkar stood opposite him. Ordered uniform. Ordered posture. Ordered silence. He was always like that. Not someone who grabbed at things in haste, but someone who looked first at how far they had already spread.

“One outer-route recovery line has been cut abnormally.”

Ater did not answer at once. Instead, he scrolled through the display with the tips of his fingers. Two gaps in the recovery route. A reassigned temporary seal number. A slight conflict in access traces. It was the texture of something erased in haste, covered in haste, and touched again in haste.

“Which route?”

“The neutral port city line.”

That was when Ater’s hand finally stopped.

Seorn did not miss the moment. It was brief, but real. For the span of a breath, his lord had stopped thinking forward and simply stared.

“It is not an ordinary disposal matter,” Seorn added. “A reading line passed through first, and a sealing line covered it afterward. The order is wrong.”

Ater closed the display in silence.

The Imperial Approval Office trusted order above all else. What was opened, what was closed, what was erased, what was left behind. That entire system began with sequence. Which meant that when sequence itself was distorted, it was worse than an ordinary mistake.

“Prepare an approach line to the site.”

Seorn bowed his head slightly.

“Official inspection, or unofficial verification?”

Before answering, Ater let his gaze settle once on the smooth face of the closed record plate. Under the polished surface, the trace of something hurriedly removed still lingered.

“For now, verification.”

Seorn stored those words, *for now*, away with care. Ater always used exact language. And exact language, with him, usually arrived half a step after the real judgment had already formed.

“Understood.”
