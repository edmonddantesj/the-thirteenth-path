# Episode 84. The Order That Cut the Market in Two

When the moment comes where method alone cannot hold,
what's needed next isn't a better method.

It's the decision of a person who chooses
what to abandon and what to save.

In Hazran,
Zahir was the one who made that decision.

As the front line inside the market was pushed back section by section,
what grew faster than the enemy's inward speed was the look on people's faces.
Everyone knew now.
This wasn't a single commotion that ends if you hold.
One bad fold,
and the entire market tears apart from the inside.

Harun was still holding the corridor front line.
Sern and Ater were still slowing the wave one section at a time.
Nasim was pushing evacuees inside, his voice going nearly hoarse.

But Zahir
was watching all of it from one step removed.

Not to run.

He was looking from a height where he could see the whole board—
where giving up which part
would let the rest continue alive.

He spoke, very low.

"Cut the central market line."

At first,
no one understood what that meant right away.

Nasim was the first to look up.

"What?"

Zahir spoke again without turning his gaze.

"Abandon the central market line."
He said.
"Sever the inner power conduit. Close the west corridor."

The moment those words fell,
the surrounding air went cold at once.

The central market line wasn't a simple thoroughfare.
It was the widest current through which Hazran breathed like Hazran—
the trunk where the most goods, people, and rumors passed through the market.
Cutting it
wasn't dividing the front line.
It was almost the same as saying *tear the city in half*.

Nasim showed edge toward Zahir for the first time.

"If you cut that, the inside gets blocked too."

"I know."
Zahir said.

"Then people can't get out either."

"That is why we pull them now."

Short. Cold.

Nasim clenched his jaw.

It wasn't wrong.
But being right made it crueler.

Harun heard that short exchange
and at first said nothing.
He was probably the person whose calculation finished fastest.

If the central market line isn't cut,
the enemy spreads deeper along the wide current.
Then the inner hull section and the evacuation corridors are both endangered.
But cut it now,
and half the market must effectively be abandoned.

It was a defensive order
and a farewell order at the same time.

Harun spoke, very low.

"It is possible."

Nasim turned to look at him.

There was anger in those eyes.
But Harun didn't look away.

"The people still on the west side, though—"
Nasim said through clenched teeth.
"We're abandoning them."

Harun answered, low.

"No."
He said.
"We have to start pulling them now."

Both were right.
That was why it was crueler.

Zahir didn't explain further.

"Nasim."
He said.
"Bring every last person from the west."

"Harun."

"Understood."
Harun answered, short.

"Sion."

Sion realized a beat late that he had been called.

"Stay between the inside and the west."
Zahir said.
"Keep them connected without gaps."

Sion nodded immediately.

There was no time to ask more.

Seorin was already moving.
She ran toward the west corridor first without a word.
Luhai followed.
Nasim bit down on a single curse and ran to scrape together the people.

Sion had expected the market to grow louder,
but it was the opposite.

When a truly bad order drops,
people go quieter for a moment.

Inside that quiet,
Hazran began cutting its own body apart.

Kael immediately helped the people tearing out the binding plates from the central column.
Ater briefly pointed out where to sever the power conduit so the enemy couldn't use it in reverse.
Sern was still buying time at the front line.
Harun kept shifting corridor angles so Sern could buy even one more beat.

In the inner section,
Jiwoo pressed her lips hard, listening to the sounds from outside.

She knew too.
What they were severing out there right now.
And that it was, in the end, the price being paid to buy time to save this ship.

Aka spoke, very low.

"It has to be split to survive."

Jiwoo didn't reply right away.

Instead she pressed her hand harder on the trembling joint.

"I know."
She said, low.
"That's why I hate it more."

The west corridor was already chaos.

When Sion arrived,
three people still there hadn't given up their goods.
Nasim was practically ripping the bundles away and throwing them.
Luhai was shoving two children through on either side, clearing the path.
Seorin, hearing the metallic sounds approaching from behind, was nearly pushing people's backs forward.

"If you don't go now, you get sealed in with it."
Seorin said, sharp.

That wasn't a threat.
It was just fact.

In the distance,
Harun's side raised the signal to clear the last angle of the central line.

Almost no time left.

Sion looked back one last time.

In the center of Hazran's market,
the central trunk where countless goods had passed, rumors had passed, people had laughed and fought and set prices—
was now preparing to sever itself.

The scene was so strange
it felt like a dream.

But war always
turns reality into something dreamlike, then collects the price of that dream for real.

With one order from Zahir,
Hazran was now
trying to split its own heart in two to survive.
