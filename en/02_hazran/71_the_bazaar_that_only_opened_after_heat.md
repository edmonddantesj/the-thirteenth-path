# Episode 71. The Bazaar That Only Opened After Heat

As the sun began to tilt, the scrap market started pulling out a face entirely different from the day's.

Sion was briefly confused whether it was even the same place.

Hazran by day was heat and metal dust and glare first. The wind struck tent edges before faces, and derelict wreckage was closer to threat than shade. But as the red sun dropped lower and the scorching metal surfaces began slowly cooling, everything that had looked merely rough by day began serving a different purpose.

Thin metal strips draped over tent covers made very light sounds each time the wind touched them. The torn heat-blocking cloth caught the evening light and glowed like a thin red membrane. Things that by day had looked like broken hull frames became, in the hours crossing into night, pillars for hanging lanterns, screening walls, alley entrance markers.

Metal piles that had been torn apart and hauled during the day now stood again as the bazaar's bones come evening.

Sion let out a very small breath watching it.

Hazran was more beautiful than he had thought.

Not a neat, clean beauty, of course. There were patching marks. Worn grain. Light barely gathered from things that had survived the day.

But that was why he kept looking longer.

Luhai, as though he had been waiting for that reaction, took the lead and spoke.

"You lose out seeing only the daytime."
He said with a grin.
"The real bazaar doesn't open until the sun's died down a bit."

Seorin said from right beside him.

"You always talk like you own the place."

Luhai shrugged.

"Don't have to be the owner. Can still be someone who loves it."

He tossed the words lightly, but strangely they did not sound hollow.

Sion chewed on that, then finally asked.

"What's so great about it."

Luhai looked around once before answering. A face that said: why are you asking something so obvious.

"Everyone looks at this place like a junkyard."
He said.
"But the really precious stuff is always hiding in places like this."

He gestured at a nearby tent. Inside, things that might have been spices or dried herbs were layered in shallow dishes, and beside them sat small oil lamps that looked recast from broken glass shards, holding red and blue light. A little deeper in, decorative plates hammered from heat-stressed metal strips, an old compass fragment that looked more expensive than water, and machine parts that a first glance would call scrap but a knowing eye would price again—all arranged together.

Luhai kept talking.

"During the day everyone just wears their surviving face. But come evening, the things here start showing a little of their real face."

Sion heard that and looked at the bazaar again.

He was right.

By day, even the same tent had looked like just a trading post. But now every alley had a hidden rhythm. Somewhere, a sound like a small string instrument trembled low. Somewhere, a water seller clinked thin metal cups to call people over. The thin scent of incense mixed with dried fruit, cooling metal, and dust—strangely, none of them was fully pushed aside by the others.

This was not a place that pretended to be beautiful. Instead, it felt like a place where surviving things slowly reclaimed their own light come evening.

Today they did not move as one group.

Gathering what was needed meant splitting paths. Jiwoo was still inside, re-matching the material list with Aka. Kael had gone ahead toward the section with hull skin metals and replacement parts. Ater and Sern had circled into another alley to check the return line situation, Harun's movements, and how far Zahir's hand had pressed order back into place today.

So the ones walking this alley together right now were Sion, Seorin, and Luhai—who had naturally attached himself on his own feet.

Seorin said low.

"Why are you still tagging along so naturally."

Luhai smiled.

"Walking alone now would be even more suspicious."

"You've been suspicious from the start."

"Fair point."

Sion listened to their exchange and smirked.

Luhai, as though he had calculated even that reaction from Sion, got even more excited and took the lead.

"This way."
He said.
"Looks like nothing from the outside, but the inner alleys are the real deal."

The alley he led them into was a place they would have passed by during the day, thinking it was just a corridor behind folded tent covers. But a few more steps inside, and a bazaar far deeper and quieter than the outside lay hidden.

Above, thin cloth and metal netting overlapped to half-cover the sky. Between them, red light and small lantern glow mixed and dappled the alley floor. On both sides, small shops built from patched cloth and metal panels pressed close together, and between them—water, incense, glass, herbs, parts, ornaments, old engraving plates—all jumbled together.

Sion looked at the sight and lost his words for a moment.

This was the same place as the daytime scrap market, yet also an entirely different one.

Luhai shrugged.

"See."
He said.
"Not bragging for nothing."

Seorin glanced at him.

"Bold words from someone who's been here a day."

Luhai did not smile immediately this time.

Instead, he said a little more quietly.

"Sometimes one day shows you more."
He said.
"People from outside all see this place as dirty, loud, and dangerous. But even in a place like that, there are things they never threw away. Kept to the end."

He looked at a tent at the alley's end—one where half-broken glass pieces were hung like candles.

"I like places like that."

Those words were far more sincere than expected.

Hearing them, Sion thought he understood a little why Luhai took pride in this place.

Hazran was not a neat place. But when it came to picking true value back out from among discarded things, it might be more honest than anywhere else.

Seorin heard those words too, but this time did not immediately mock.

Instead, she said low.

"It's worth bragging about."

Luhai widened his eyes slightly at that, then quickly smiled.

"Oh, hearing that from you—that's worth a lot."

"Don't remember it too long."

"That's cold."

Sion watched the two of them go back and forth, and felt a sudden premonition that this moment would stay with him longer than expected.

The red light before the sun fully faded. The lanterns beneath patched tent covers. The alleys where surviving things slowly reclaimed their faces come evening. And Luhai, showing it off like a point of pride.

Before leaving, this felt like a scene that only someone who had properly looked at this place—not merely passed through—could carry away.

And in the middle of it, Sion heard a very close footstep beside him.

Seorin.

The distance he had always known. But tonight, strangely, it felt more vivid than during the day.

A distance he had grown so used to that he had let it pass without thought. But beneath the evening light, it felt like even that familiarity could carry one more name.

Sion looked straight ahead for no reason.

Seorin said nothing.

Instead, at the alley's end, the light of a small swaying glass lantern grazed past the side of her face.

Sion saw it, and could not look away.
