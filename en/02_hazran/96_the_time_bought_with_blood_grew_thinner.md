# Episode 96. The Time Bought with Blood Grew Thinner

When the second group ended,
Hazran had clearly pushed a few more people outside again.

But proportionally,
the time left inside didn't grow thicker.
It grew thinner.

Sion could tell immediately.

As people left, the area near the hull should have emptied a little.
But the reality was the opposite.
More share landed on the remaining people's shoulders.
The line grew thinner.
The hands that had to hold got fewer.

Time bought with blood
does not accumulate.

Each time it's spent, it gets thinner.

The same went for the blood that flowed from Harun's arm.

The cloth hastily wrapped around it was already half-soaked.
He was now using his left almost only as a brace.
As Ater had said, he stopped receiving head-on,
shifting to twisting his body, deflecting angles—changed his movement entirely.

But no matter how,
a bleeding body cannot stand at the same intensity for long.

Sern watched it with a face like he was biting the inside of his lip hard.
He was injured too.
So he knew better.
That was no longer a stage where enduring alone was enough.

Ater cut his words down even further.

Only what was needed.

"Second unit on the right—vision shaking."
"Abandon the left barrier once more."
"Harun, back for now."

Between those short words,
how precariously the front line was hanging was laid bare.

At some point, Harun had nearly stopped answering.

He just moved.

That was more unsettling.

Sion, moving between the front line and the hull,
had to force himself to tear his eyes away from Harun's side.

If he kept watching that man,
the line here would break.
If the line here broke,
the meaning of that man holding on while bleeding would vanish.

So he kept moving.

Counting the next line.
Clearing things that could trip feet.
Pushing with his shoulder so the evacuation line didn't lean to one side.
Tamping the sand beneath the hull ramp again.

Seorin was the same.

By now her hands moved before her words.
Turning people's shoulders.
Adjusting stretcher angles.
Catching a child about to fall.
Kicking luggage aside with her foot.

But Sion knew.

The faster her hands got,
the more quietly the inside was breaking.

Luhai's face was drenched with sweat, out of breath,
but he kept counting heads.

"Three left."
He said, low.
"Three more still."

Nasim took it in his hoarse voice immediately.

"Then pull three."

The most obvious thing in the world.
But right now Hazran
was burning human blood, metal, and time together
to make that obvious thing real.

Jiwoo watched the third group's boarding preparation and her face hardened.

The ship was still holding.
But holding also meant
each new weight entering from now
was shaving that hold down.

Aka, listening to the hull's trembling,
spoke very low.

"The third can't take a long intake."

Jiwoo asked immediately.

"How many at once."

Aka closed her eyes briefly, then said.

"Short bursts."
"A long line shakes it all together."

Meaning
it was no longer about loading more people at once.
They had to cut into short clusters and board quickly.

Jiwoo decided at once.

"Third group—three at a time!"
She shouted.
"Don't line them up long!"

Nasim heard that and immediately rearranged the queue.

"Groups of three! First three, ready!"

The people left inside Hazran
were now compressed to the point where they could barely tell
if they were a line,
a cluster,
or simply a body to be pushed up next.

But through it all,
people climbed.

Because they had to.

Behind the right corridor,
the fight to lock the tilted breacher into a permanent obstacle continued.

Sern targeted the rear reinforcement axis again.
Ater kept tearing at the angles and vision lines
to prevent the regular units from climbing over the body.
Harun, with his remaining arm and his whole body,
held the collapsed weight twisted so it couldn't flatten the path again.

But Sion could see it now.

Harun's movement had slowed—very faintly.
One hold,
then the interval to the next hold slightly longer than before.
No one said it aloud,
but it was the kind of change that shows too clearly on a battlefield.

And right then,
Ater raised his voice slightly for the first time.

"Next press incoming."

Sern spoke almost simultaneously.

"This time it is not only the right."

Sion's breath caught.

That was worse.
While Harun was already bleeding and holding on one side,
if another corridor shook at the same time,
the entire last survival zone near the hull could collapse together.

Zahir heard that
and closed his eyes for the briefest moment before opening them again.

His face held no hesitation.
But instead,
it held the calculation of a man who knows how few moves remain.

"Attach the third group,"
he said, low,
"and then redistribute the remaining hands."

The moment those words fell,
Sion felt his chest grow heavier.

*Remaining hands.*

People were now being counted
not by names or relationships
but by time remaining and capacity to hold.

War always pushes that far.

And Hazran
had arrived at exactly that point.
