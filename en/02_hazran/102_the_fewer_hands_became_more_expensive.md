# Episode 102. The Fewer Hands Became More Expensive

After the fourth group began to move,
the inside of Hazran grew quieter than before.

Growing quiet in the middle of a loud battlefield
isn't a good sign.

It means everyone
has cut the breath they spend on unnecessary words.

Sion felt the shift immediately.

Nasim's voice was still cracked,
but it no longer crashed over people the way it used to.
Now only the necessary words came out, torn.

"Front three."
"Rear, hold."
"Now."

Seorin stopped explaining too.
She set angles with her hands,
cut lines,
and split those who should move from those who should stop with her eyes alone.
Luhai held the shoulders of anyone crying or afraid
and simply pushed them forward.

Everyone
had begun stripping down to only the essential movements.

Not because the fight had gone on too long,
but because each remaining hand had become too expensive.

If even one more person here gets hurt or falls,
it isn't simply one person dropping out.
The time that person was carrying,
the line that person was holding,
the gap that person was filling—all go empty together.

Hazran was now
counting people not by headcount
but by positions that must not be vacated.

That fact kept choking Sion's breath.

In the right corridor, after Harun went down once,
Sern and Ater were truly filling that spot together.

Harun was standing again.
But anyone could see
it wasn't the same body as before.
His left arm hung lower.
His breathing had shortened.
Instead of receiving forward weight, he only held by deflecting angles.

Sern had to run more to cover the gap.
Unable to even hide his wounded side,
short cut, immediate pullout,
drive in again,
pull out again.

Ater watched both and threw only necessary words.

"Sern, below."
"Harun, back for now."
"Upper right, opening again."

No urgency in that voice.
Which made it more precarious.
It was the voice of someone who had to stay precise for too long
without even the freedom to be rushed.

Kael on the left was no different.

He wasn't holding alone,
but the fact that *the left hadn't collapsed since Kael entered*
showed exactly how expensive that one hand was.

Kael was halfway through raising a low metal barrier again
when he spoke, brief.

"One more hit and I drop this."

Sion understood immediately.

Dropping the barrier means abandoning the space behind it.
Abandoning the space means
pressing people even further into the inside.

The last space near the hull was already packed.
But it could still shrink more.
The thought alone was suffocating.

Jiwoo, receiving the fourth group's first three,
was reading the hull entrance's trembling through her hand.

"Less shaking this time."
She said, low.

Aka replied at once.

"Fewer people."

That was fact.

When the size of each cluster shrinks,
the ship shakes less.
But from the system's side that's stability;
from the human side, it's an even crueler division.

To survive,
people now had to be cut into even smaller pieces to be loaded.

Jiwoo exhaled something that was almost a laugh.

"Not sure if I should call that good."

Aka didn't answer.
She placed her hand on the hull's outer wall instead,
listening for the next trembling first.

Zahir watched everyone
and continued preparing to redistribute hands the moment the fourth group ended.

This time it was more open.

He was already counting people with his eyes.
Who was still walking.
Who could still run.
Who needed to be pulled now.

Each time Sion caught that gaze,
he felt himself being counted as one hand too.

It felt wrong,
but it couldn't be denied.
Because this was truly that stage now.

That moment,
Luhai called out low.

"One person slowing!"

Someone at the back of the fourth line couldn't lift their legs properly,
and the line jammed again briefly.

Before Sion could run over,
Seorin had already moved to that person's side.

Without a word, she lifted the person's waist
and pushed them one section forward.
The motion was neither too rough
nor too gentle,
which made it more like Seorin.

"Go."
She said, low.

One syllable.

But right now, that one syllable was enough.

The line moved again.

Even that short restart
meant one more unit of time for Hazran.

And Sion understood.

The battle was no longer heading toward grander heroic scenes.
It was holding on by how many more of these small restarts could be made.

Each shrinking hand grew more expensive,
and so the remaining people
had no choice but to move more quietly, more precisely, more ruthlessly.

Hazran was now,
with those costly hands,
barely pushing the next few people outside again.
