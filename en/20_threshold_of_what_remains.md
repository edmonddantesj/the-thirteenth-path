# Episode 20. Threshold of What Remains

Han Jiwoo's ship was half-wedged against the outer wall of the sealed structure.

More precisely, rather than a proper dock, the hull was forced into a precarious perch between the severed outer wall and a remaining connection ring. A slight shift in angle would scrape against the wall and slide off; pull back too far and they'd lose the reaction zone Sion was reading. The ship wasn't attached to the structure—it was holding on to keep from falling apart.

Sion had his body low near the hull's side slit, looking outward.

This place was neither fully interior nor fully exposed to space. Between the severed outer wall and the half-collapsed corridor, a containment field that should have died long ago still barely persisted. Cold and thin, but definitely breathable air; an external junction that felt like it would drop you into the void with one wrong step; and beyond that, the shadow of a semi-open structure leading inward. The threshold of a sealed transfer point floating in space, still not quite dead.

Seorin, leaning against the inner hull wall, spoke.

"Judging by that look on your face, you're not exactly thrilled."

Sion replied without taking his eyes off the outside.

"Why would I be. When something you thought was dead is only half alive, it's always more of a pain."

A low burst of static swept through the short-range closed channel.

Every time the metal layers between the structure's outer wall and the hull overlapped, the local channel Han Jiwoo had set up picked up those micro-interruptions. It couldn't reach far. Just enough to link whoever stayed on the ship with whoever went outside—barely.

Han Jiwoo's voice came through.

"Push the hull angle any more and it'll scrape."

Sion said immediately.

"This is the limit. Push harder and we're dead."

Ater moved to Sion's side and examined the severed alignment lines on the structure's outer wall. The metal had been cut once long ago, then killed again with a second layer patched over it. This wasn't a structure that had been sealed from the start. Someone had tried to block this path, and someone else had kept it from fully dying. The gap between those layers was so stark it felt ominous.

Sern, reading the external load distribution, spoke quietly.

"The outer wall holds. The problem is inside. If there are live reactions, whatever dies first when disturbed will likely be on the inside."

Ater added in a very low voice.

"This might not be a door-opening mechanism, but a visitor-selection mechanism."

At that, Sion's eyes narrowed slightly.

Beyond the slit, in the darkness, a very faint tremor was rising from beneath the severed ring. Too regular for dead metal reflection, too weak for a live guidance line. Not a signal calling someone to come, but closer to a reaction still checking whether whoever arrived was qualified.

Han Jiwoo widened the external view slightly.

A broken bridge, twisted cables, a sealed dock ring, and behind it all, half-buried in shadow, a small fixed structure. At first glance it looked like an abandoned access fitting. But on closer inspection, the core junction points were intact to an suspicious degree. Not collapsed from explosion or decay—more like everything around the important parts had been deliberately cut away.

Sion said very quietly.

"There it is."

Han Jiwoo acknowledged briefly.

"Yeah. The first remaining marker."

Sern said immediately.

"It was severed."

Ater, looking at the same point, spoke.

"Most likely a secondary discrimination unit that once regulated approach to the outer transfer point."

Seorin frowned.

"Great. So it's not just a door, then."

"Right."
Sion said.
"If it opened for anyone who walked up, it would've died a long time ago."

That connected precisely to why they had come this far. The name-fragment Elia read didn't just point to one person's false conviction—Jun Aster's. It had left behind the sensation that the entire path that name was supposed to reach had been severed somewhere. And the structure before them now was most likely the first physical evidence that such a path had actually existed.

This was not a simple ruin expedition.

If they could properly read the remaining reactions here, they would obtain the first piece of proof that the Jun Aster case wasn't just one person's incineration. If they disturbed things wrong, the remaining reactions would die, and the first fragment line would sever with them. Worse, the discrimination unit's final pulse could leak to an external tracking network.

Seorin asked briefly.

"Going in?"

Han Jiwoo adjusted the hull angle just slightly lower and answered.

"Can't dock directly. There's a still-active catch point below that structure. Need to check that first."

"Chance of a trap?"
Sern asked.

"High."
Han Jiwoo answered casually.
"But trap or not, if we don't look, there's no next step."

A short silence.

They could turn back. But then the reason they came this far would be severed along with the retreat. If they pulled away without examining this threshold, they'd most likely lose forever the first empirical link between the name fragment, the coordinates, and the dead marker reactions.

Sion pressed the outside of his inner pocket once.

The fragment bearing Jun Aster's name. And the first trace of the erased path that had led them here. If both truly belonged to the same event, then the structure before them might be something like the opening sentence connecting the two.

Han Jiwoo let the ship drift forward at a crawl without fully stopping.

A full stop would be more likely to trigger external surveillance. This area was a dead route, but not an empty one. Depending on who read the markers first, the path could open or die.

Ater said quietly.

"There must be an approach sequence."

Han Jiwoo heard that and smirked.

"Obviously. That's why we didn't come head-on."

Ater kept his gaze fixed and continued.

"That structure is the remains of a docking-approach guidance ring. But the core guidance line was killed, and only the secondary alignment unit was left. Someone cut it so that only the informed could approach."

The cabin went quiet for a moment.

Sion heard that and looked at Ater once. He himself saw the grain first; the other saw the structure first. But right now, both were converging on the same answer to an unsettling degree.

Sern added quietly.

"Then the remaining ring is more likely a discrimination device than an entry device."

Seorin said briefly.

"Great. So it screens you before it lets you in."

"Which means if you just walk up, you die."
This time it was Sion who followed up, not Han Jiwoo.

The moment those words landed, the air inside the cabin changed again.

What they were about to do was no longer simple approach. It was reading the surviving reactions without destroying them and making the discrimination unit recognize that they were not the wrong party. Not moving slowly, but stopping to avoid being wrong.

Han Jiwoo lifted one hand from the control stick and spoke.

"Fine. Here's how we do it. I hold the ship's angle. Sion reads the trace first. Sern calculates the structural sequence. Ater figures out what the original approach logic was. Seorin cuts us off if anything goes wrong."

The instruction was brief, and oddly natural. Nobody had been named leader, yet in this moment everyone knew it was the most fitting arrangement.

Seorin gave a very short smile.

"Now it feels like real work."

Sion leaned closer to the slit.

The structure's surface was almost dead, but not entirely. Beneath where the severed guidance line ended, a very faint reaction still lingered. Not waiting to be read—closer to checking whether whoever arrived was reading correctly, ready to die the instant the answer was wrong.

Ater froze his breathing the moment he saw the pattern.

"This is…"

Sern immediately shifted his gaze.

"Do you recognize it?"

A few seconds later, Ater spoke in a low, rigid voice.

"It resembles Imperial sealing logic, but it's not quite the same. It's older."

At that moment, Sion spotted a very faint scratch resembling a mark beneath the structure. It could have been a cat-folk lineage symbol, or something even older. Too worn for certainty, but one thing was clear.

This wasn't an abandoned path.
It was a path left to look abandoned.
Erased yet never fully killed—a path someone had deliberately made readable only from what remained.

Han Jiwoo said quietly.

"Then now we really dock."

The ship slid forward at a glacial pace.

As the distance between structure and hull closed, no one inside the cabin spoke. Each was reading the same threshold in their own way. Sion read the grain, Ater the logic, Sern the sequence, Seorin the moment to cut, and Han Jiwoo the angle that kept them alive.

And the moment all five senses converged on a single point for the first time, the first real threshold of the erased path began to open quietly before them.

From here on, the cost of failure changed too. Getting caught wouldn't just mean running again—the first crime scene that could reveal who severed this path would die. So this approach was not exploration; it was the first discrimination that would set the price of everything to follow.
