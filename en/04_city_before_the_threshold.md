# Episode 4. The City Before the Threshold

The upper connector bridges of the neutral port city were far quieter than the lower levels.

Though they belonged to the same city, the sounds changed completely from one layer to another. Below, curses, transactions, and metal noise collided all at once. Above, footsteps and closing doors stood out with sharper clarity. The more expensive the information or the cargo became, the less anyone said aloud.

Ater Valkar was more familiar with that upper air.

The hem of his black coat shifted lightly in the wind running through the bridge. Even beneath the coarse lighting of the port, his stride never lost its order. The neutral port city liked to pretend it existed outside structure, but Ater knew better. Places like this did not have fewer rules. They only had rules written by different hands.

One step behind him, Seorn spoke in a low voice.

“The disturbance at the outer docking tier has already spread. One limping courier, two gray-coated pursuers, and a third party that intercepted the object in the middle.”

Without turning, Ater asked,

“A third party.”

“That is how the port is describing them.”
Seorn paused, then added,
“Most likely an unstructured hand.”

At those words, Ater’s pace slowed by the smallest degree.
Seorn did not miss even that.

“The tag. Was it recovered?”

“We still do not know whose hand it ended up in.
But the courier definitely lost it.”

Ater looked out through the glass wall at the lower levels of the port.
Even now the gray crowd below was in constant motion. One disturbance vanished quickly beneath another noise. A person disappeared here as easily as a few cargo crates going missing. That was the nature of the neutral port city.

“And the archivist?”

“The stronger rumor says he is still alive.”

Ater’s gaze sank a little.

“Rumors tend to arrive last.”

“Yes.
But this one feels as though someone is delaying it on purpose.”

“Why do you think that?”

Seorn activated a small terminal.
Statements from port informants, chatter from outer-route crew, traces from the pawn quarter, and one short phrase repeating across them all.

**Delivery on hold pending confirmation that the archivist is alive.**

“The same line is being repeated by different mouths,” Seorn said quietly.
“This does not look like a courier delaying on his own judgment. It looks like someone above him is making him wait for proof that the archivist is alive.”

Ater glanced at the display once and then shut it again.

“Then the archivist himself still matters more than the object.”

“At least for now.”

“That is not good.”

Seorn did not spell out the meaning hidden inside that short sentence.
Whenever a person became more important than the record, the situation grew dangerous. People disappeared later than records did, and always moved in more unpredictable ways.

They crossed the upper bridge and entered a narrow passage between the port’s formal administrative wing and its civilian storage quarter.
This was where legitimate lanes and illegitimate holding lines overlapped. Officially, nothing was traded here. In reality, more information passed through this space than through most legal channels. If Ater revealed the Valkar name, doors would open, but so would too many watching eyes.

Seorn continued,

“If the tag is gone, all that remains is the delivery point.”

“Candidates?”

“Three.”

A thin projection opened in the air.
A lower temporary lodging block. A rear warehouse behind the loading berths. And an archive-ruin access bridge that had been closed long ago, though now it existed under a different name.

Ater’s eyes stopped on the third.

Seeing that, Seorn added quietly,

“That is the one I find most unpleasant as well.”

“Why?”

“It is inconvenient if you want to hide a person.
And almost ideal if you want to hide a record.”

Ater thought a moment, then asked,

“If they intend to keep the archivist alive, where would they place him closest to?”

“Near what remains of the record.”

“Then the answer is decided.”

Seorn nodded.

“The R-12 ruin access bridge.”

The moment he finished speaking, the sound of someone running rang up from below.
Their attention shifted downward together.

Two unfamiliar movements cut through the crowd below.
One had the rough stride of a field worker. The other moved half a beat behind, carving sightlines apart with sharper steps. Too alert to be dock labor. Too quick at reading the space around them.

Seorn spoke first.

“The Alliance Outer Route Bureau side.”

Ater did not answer.

The two figures passed through the lower level too quickly to see clearly, but something about them stayed in the eye. One moved as if reading the air before the evidence. The other moved as if reading the first person’s breaking point.

An unstructured hand.

Very slowly, Ater drew his gaze back.

“Faster than expected.”

Seorn read the shape hidden inside those words.
His lord was irritated.
But a little more than that, he was curious.

“Shall we make contact?”

“Not yet.”

“Then follow only?”

“No.”

The answer was short and clear.

“Let them go to the same place.”

Seorn ran through the meaning once in silence.
If they seized them too early, the whole port would shake. Better to let all sides converge at the same threshold and see who tried to open what first.

“Understood.”

Ater resumed walking.
The R-12 ruin access bridge.
A route closed, yet not wholly dead.
A poor place to hide a living man, and almost for that reason the best possible place to hide what mattered.

Meanwhile below, Sion had just finished reading the sign at the end of the alley.

**R-12 Ruin Access Bridge. Outer access prohibited.**

He turned the tag once more in his hand.
The short projection they had just read and the sign in front of him matched each other too neatly.

Seorin looked at the sign as well.

“Nice.
At this point it’s basically begging people not to come.”

“Which is exactly why it’s probably the right place.”

“Why does life only work like that for you?”

“A bloodhound works beside warning signs.”

Seorin laughed under her breath.

“That’s not a profession. That’s a personality defect.”

Sion traced the locking mechanism below the sign and found a recent scratch almost immediately.
Not the mark of a door long sealed, but of one that had been forced open and shut again not long ago.

He drew in a short breath.

“This is it.”

“You sure?”

“Not sure,” Sion said quietly.
“But close enough that I’m about to be.”

At that same moment, on the bridge above, Ater also stopped walking.
Two figures stood by the lock below. Too far away to make out their faces, but one of them was very clearly standing in the posture of someone who read traces before anything else.

Seorn asked softly,

“You saw them.”

“Yes.”

“One Alliance Outer Route Bureau field worker and one assistant, by the look of it.”

Ater did not answer right away.
His gaze moved slowly over the lock, the two shadows standing before it, and the dark access bridge continuing behind them.

“Assistant,” he said at last.

Seorn heard the word catch slightly in his throat.

“That one does not move like an assistant.”

Seorn nearly smiled then. His lord had seen it too.
One of them was reading the site. The other was already watching where that person might break.

A beat later Ater answered,

“We wait until the door opens.”

And at that exact moment,
from the darkness inside the R-12 ruin access bridge,
a single low metallic sound rang out.

Like a sign that someone inside had not yet completely left.

Sion and Seorin, Ater and Seorn,
had reached the same threshold at last,
without even properly knowing each other’s faces.
